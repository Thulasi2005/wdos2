GREEN TURTLES


In Sri Lankan seas, the green turtle is the most prevalent. They got their name from the green tint of their fat. They are herbivores and mostly eat algae and sea grass. Female Green turtles come ashore to lay their eggs on the beaches during the nesting season. A mature female may lay between 120 and 40 eggs at a time, and she can grow to a maximum length of 1m and weigh around 250kg. In Sri Lanka, turtle eggs are now protected by hatcheries from both predators and people.


The southern coast, the eastern coast, and the west coast of Sri Lanka are just a few locations where you might see green turtles.


  • Rekawa Turtle Conservation:
    -This conservation initiative, which is situated on Sri Lanka's south coast, is well-known for its night patrols, during which time guests may see Green turtles laying their eggs.
  • Mirissa Beach:
    -Mirissa Beach, which lies on Sri Lanka's south coast, is a well-liked location for surfing and whale viewing. Visitors may also see green turtles laying their eggs on the beach.

The hawksbill's narrow head and bird-like beak, which are designed to trap creatures hidden in small cracks, are how it got its name. Despite being smaller, this species is renowned for its exquisite shell, which is composed of 13 symmetrical, vibrant pieces.


They can be found all over Sri Lanka, including on the southern, eastern, and western coasts.

Pigeon Island National Park:
This national park, which lies off the coast of Sri Lanka, is a well-liked place to go diving and snorkelling. Hawksbill turtles can be seen by visitors while swimming in the clean waters.

LOGGERHEAD TURTLES


The Loggerhead Turtle is easily recognized by its distinctively big head and is often coloured red and brown. These are primarily carnivores that can reach a maximum height of 1 m and weigh between 170 and 200 kg. Their large, powerful jaws are perfect for crushing mollusks and crustaceans, which are their favourite food. The degradation of its nesting places and other human activities like bycatch have made the loggerhead turtle a critically endangered species in Sri Lanka.


They can be found all over Sri Lanka, mainly on western coasts.



  • Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery:
    -This hatchery, which is situated on Sri Lanka's west coast, is devoted to the preservation and protection of all five species of sea turtles that can be found there. Visitors are welcome to learn about the conservation efforts of the hatchery and even release newborn turtles into the ocean.
