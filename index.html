There are 250 different species of turtles in the world. Sea turtles, which are seven of them, are marine life. The remaining 243 species are terrestrial or dwell in freshwater marshes and ponds. Tortoises are the name for turtles that only live on land, or terrestrial turtles. The coastlines of Sri Lanka are visited by five of the world's seven species of sea turtles during the breeding season. They are the Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea), Green (Chelonia mydas), Hawksbill (Eretmochelys imbricata), Loggerhead (Caretta caretta), and Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea) turtles. In Tamil, the sea turtles are referred to as Amai and in Sinhala respectively, Kesbawa.




Exploring the benefits of turtles to Sri Lanka

  1. Coral reefs:
    Hawksbills live on coral reefs and are known for only consuming a few types of sea sponges. The variety of life on the reef is increased by this diet, which enables the growth of uncommon forms of sponges. Without Hawksbills, sponges might overgrow, suffocate, and kill slow-growing corals. The Hawksbill's position on the reef has grown even more important as it faces increasing threats from climate change and other negative factors.

  2. For beaches:
    Additionally, sea turtles benefit the water. By laying their eggs on the sand, sea turtles that are nesting benefit beaches. Eggshells and unhatched eggs that are left behind supply vital nutrients that feed beach grasses and other dune vegetation, which stabilizes dunes and helps prevent coastal erosion.

The impact of Sri Lankan government

  • Sea turtles have several nesting sites in Sri Lanka, however if unchecked, poaching by people, predators, and dogs can happen often. The Sri Lankan government has licensed 22 turtle hatcheries along the nation's western and southern coastlines as a precaution against this.


  • Its primary goal is to promote the sea turtles' general welfare and grow their population through conservation measures. This entails safeguarding the eggs from predators and poachers, provide a protected area for hatchlings, caring for young, ill, and damaged turtles, feeding them each day, and eventually releasing the healthy turtles back into the ocean.



Tourism:A vital tool for tutle conservation

  • Greater opportunities are provided by tourism for developing nations like Sri Lanka, helping to preserve the environment while also boosting local economies.
